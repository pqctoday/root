Canada's Approach to Post-Quantum Cryptography

While the USA through NIST leads the standardization process for post-quantum cryptography (PQC), Canada is proactively promoting the transition to a quantum-safe future by:

Key Aspects of Canada's Strategy

Raising Awareness and Promoting Knowledge

  • Canada is actively raising awareness about the quantum threat and the need for PQC among government agencies, industry stakeholders, and the general public.
  • The Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (Cyber Centre) plays a key role in this effort by promoting awareness about the impact of quantum computers on cryptography and the steps needed to prepare for the transition to quantum-resistant cryptography.

Collaborating with International Partners

  • The Cyber Centre actively participates in international standardization bodies, including NIST, the International Standards Organization, and the Internet Engineering Task Force, to ensure that new standards for quantum-resistant cryptography meet the cryptographic security and privacy needs of Canadians.

Encouraging Preparedness and Crypto-Agility

  • Canada encourages organizations to take proactive steps to manage the risks associated with quantum computing.
  • These steps include conducting risk assessments, creating cryptographic inventories, educating teams about quantum threats, engaging with vendors to understand their PQC plans, and ensuring crypto-agility to adapt to evolving standards.

Developing National Strategies and Guidelines

  • Canada has been working on national strategies and guidelines for PQC migration, focusing on critical infrastructure and government systems.
  • The Canadian Forum for Digital Infrastructure Resilience (CFDIR), a public-private partnership, has released best practices and guidelines for quantum readiness, outlining PQC use cases and a checklist for assessing PQC readiness.

Investing in Research and Development

  • Canada has been investing in quantum technologies research and development, including PQC, recognizing its importance for national security and economic prosperity.
  • Organizations like the Institute for Quantum Computing (IQC) and the Creative Destruction Lab (CDL) have been instrumental in supporting quantum research and fostering innovation in the field.

Canada's Comprehensive Approach

Building a Strong Quantum Ecosystem

  • Canada is home to a thriving quantum ecosystem, with a high density of quantum startups and a strong research community.
  • This ecosystem fosters innovation, collaboration, and knowledge sharing, contributing to the development and adoption of quantum-safe technologies, including PQC.

Addressing the Talent Gap

  • Recognizing the importance of skilled professionals in the quantum field, Canada is investing in education and training programs to develop a workforce capable of implementing and managing PQC solutions.
  • Initiatives like the graduate programs at IQC and summer schools for undergraduate students help build a talent pipeline for the quantum industry.

Collaborating with Industry Partners

  • Canada is working with industry partners to encourage the development and adoption of PQC solutions, promoting collaboration between quantum technology companies, cybersecurity firms, and organizations across various sectors.

Securing the Quantum Supply Chain

  • Canada recognizes the importance of securing the quantum supply chain to mitigate risks associated with adversarial investments and interference in the quantum ecosystem.
  • This involves assessing supply chain vulnerabilities and working with trusted allies to ensure the integrity and security of quantum technologies, including PQC.

Canada's efforts extend beyond solely focusing on technical aspects of PQC. The country recognizes the need for a comprehensive approach that addresses technical, economic, and geopolitical aspects of the quantum threat.

By focusing on raising awareness, collaborating with partners, developing national guidelines, investing in research, and fostering a strong quantum ecosystem, Canada aims to ensure a smooth and secure transition to a post-quantum future, safeguarding its critical infrastructure and sensitive data from the potential threat of quantum computers.
