South Korea's Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) Strategy

  • National Standardization through KpqC:

    South Korea has initiated its own PQC competition, known as KpqC (Korean Post-Quantum Cryptography), similar in structure to the NIST competition. The goal is to select PQC algorithms for digital signatures and key establishment/public key encryption. The first round concluded in December 2023, with four algorithms progressing in each category. The final round is anticipated to end in September 2024.

    • The KpqC competition requires proposals to meet stringent criteria: publication in a top international conference or journal, or at least in the IACR Cryptology ePrint Archive. Technical descriptions, security proofs, and a reference implementation in ANSI C are mandatory.
    • This suggests that South Korea is committed to establishing robust, independently evaluated PQC standards.
  • Government-Led Roadmap:

    The Ministry of Science and ICT has a published work plan, indicating a high-level commitment to PQC transition. While details of the roadmap's content are not provided in the sources, its existence points to a coordinated national effort.

  • Ambitious Timeline:

    South Korea aims to transform its national cryptography systems to post-quantum cryptography by 2035. This aligns with the US target for migrating federal systems.

  • Quantum-Safe Communication Infrastructure:

    In addition to PQC, South Korea is investing in quantum key distribution (QKD) as part of its "Digital New Deal" initiative.

    • Pilot quantum cryptography networks have been deployed across 26 public and private institutions. This practical experimentation suggests a focus on real-world deployment and integration of QKD.

Overall, South Korea's strategy demonstrates a proactive approach to the quantum threat. The country is not only relying on international standards but also developing its own PQC expertise and infrastructure, suggesting a desire for technological independence in this crucial domain.
