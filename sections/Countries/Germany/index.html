Germany's Approach to Post-Quantum Cryptography

Germany, primarily through its Federal Office for Information Security (BSI), is taking a proactive and multifaceted approach to promote the transition to post-quantum cryptography (PQC). The country recognizes the urgency of addressing the threat posed by cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs).

Key Aspects of Germany's Strategy

Promoting Awareness and Understanding

  • BSI published the guideline "Quantum-safe cryptography â€“ fundamentals, current developments, and recommendations" in 2021:
    • Background information on quantum computers, PQC, and QKD.
    • Insights into developments in politics, research, and industry related to quantum-safe cryptography.
    • Recommendations for actions, including preparation/inventory, cryptographic agility, and the use of hybrid solutions.
  • BSI emphasizes the need to address "store now, decrypt later" attacks and the importance of starting PQC migration immediately due to expected long migration times.
  • BSI operates under the hypothesis that cryptographically relevant quantum computers will be available in the early 2030s.
  • Advocates for cryptographic agility as a fundamental design criterion for systems.

Supporting International Standardization Efforts and Defining National Guidelines

  • Germany actively participates in the NIST PQC standardization process and the EU's NIS Cooperation Group's PQC workstream, co-chaired with France and the Netherlands, to harmonize PQC transition across the EU.
  • Supports evaluating and certifying systems not chosen by NIST but that demonstrate strong security.
  • Provides guidance on specific PQC mechanisms:
    • Recommends FrodoKEM and Classic McEliece for KEMs.
    • Plans to include ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA in the 2025 update to technical guidelines.

Prioritizing High-Security Systems and Public Administration PKI Migration

  • Germany prioritizes migrating high-security systems, particularly the Public Administration PKI ("Verwaltungs-PKI", V-PKI), which has approximately 500,000 subscribers and is used for S/MIME, TLS, and other applications.

Advocating for Hybrid Solutions and Expressing Reservations about QKD

  • BSI recommends using PQC in a hybrid mode, combining it with existing RSA or ECC algorithms to enhance security during the transition phase.
  • Expresses reservations about QKD due to its maturity and security challenges, suggesting it may serve as a complementary measure in the future but not as a sole solution.

Providing Open Source Tools and Resources

  • BSI offers Botan, an open-source cryptographic library written in C++ that supports both quantum-vulnerable and post-quantum cryptography, facilitating implementation and testing of PQC algorithms.

Germany's approach to PQC transition demonstrates a strong commitment to ensuring long-term cybersecurity in the face of quantum computing advancements. They are actively promoting awareness, providing guidance, supporting research, developing tools, and collaborating with national and international partners to ensure a smooth and secure migration to a quantum-resistant future.
