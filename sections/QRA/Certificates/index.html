Pure PQC Certificates

Pure Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) certificates are designed to be secure against quantum computer attacks. They use algorithms that are believed to be resistant to the computational power of quantum computers, ensuring long-term security for digital communications.

Hybrid Certificates

Hybrid certificates combine traditional cryptographic algorithms with post-quantum algorithms. This approach provides a transitional solution, offering security against both classical and quantum attacks. Hybrid certificates ensure compatibility with existing systems while preparing for future quantum threats.

Composite Certificates

Composite certificates use multiple cryptographic algorithms simultaneously. They provide a higher level of security by requiring an attacker to break all included algorithms to compromise the certificate. This method enhances robustness against various types of cryptographic attacks.

Other Options

Other options for digital certificates include traditional certificates that use well-established cryptographic algorithms such as RSA and ECC. These certificates are widely used and trusted for securing digital communications, but they may not be secure against future quantum attacks.

The Quantum Computing Threat

The rapid advancement of quantum computing poses a significant threat to current digital certificate infrastructures, which rely heavily on public-key cryptography vulnerable to quantum attacks. To address this challenge, various formats of digital certificates are being explored and developed to ensure security in the post-quantum era.

Digital Certificate Formats

Pure PQC Certificates

These certificates exclusively utilize post-quantum cryptography (PQC) algorithms for key exchange and digital signatures. They offer the highest level of protection against quantum attacks but may face challenges regarding interoperability with existing systems during the transition phase.

Hybrid Certificates

These certificates combine traditional (quantum-vulnerable) algorithms with PQC algorithms. This approach provides a balance between security and compatibility, enabling a smoother transition to a quantum-safe environment. It allows legacy systems to continue functioning while incorporating quantum-resistant elements. However, hybrid certificates may potentially increase the attack surface as they increase the complexity of the solution.

Composite Certificates

Composite certificates, currently under development by the IETF, offer a more sophisticated approach to hybrid certificates. They allow for the inclusion of multiple signatures generated by different algorithms within a single certificate. This approach provides flexibility in supporting diverse algorithms, including both traditional and PQC options, facilitating a gradual migration to PQC.

Catalyst Certificates

The ITU-T has standardized a variant of hybrid certificates called catalyst certificates. Catalyst certificates support multiple algorithms within a single certificate, allowing for a flexible transition to PQC.

Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM)

CBOMs, based on the CycloneDX Software Bill of Materials (SBOMs), provide a machine-readable standardized format for documenting cryptographic assets. They offer a comprehensive inventory of cryptographic elements, including algorithms, keys, certificates, and their dependencies, which is essential for assessing quantum vulnerability and planning a PQC migration.

Benefits and Challenges

Format Benefits Challenges
Pure PQC Highest quantum security, forward-looking approach Interoperability issues, limited support in current systems, potential performance overhead
Hybrid Balanced security and compatibility, facilitates a smoother transition, leverages existing infrastructure Potential increase in attack surface, added complexity, may require larger certificates
Composite Flexibility and gradual migration, supports diverse algorithms, allows for future-proofing Standardization in progress, implementation complexity, may require updates to existing systems
CBOM Comprehensive inventory of cryptographic assets, facilitates quantum vulnerability assessment, aids in migration planning Requires adoption of standardized format, implementation and maintenance effort, may require integration with existing security tools

Standards and Standardization Efforts

Standardized

FIPS 140-2: The current standard for cryptography already permits the use of hybrid schemes, enabling certification for systems using both traditional and PQC algorithms.

Draft or Final Stages

ITU-T X.509v3: The ITU-T has standardized a format for hybrid certificates (catalyst certificates) in Section 9.8 of its X.509v3 standard.

IETF Internet Drafts: The IETF is actively developing internet drafts proposing composite certificates as an alternative form of hybrid certificates. Two relevant drafts are:

  • draft-ietf-lamps-pq-composite-kem-04: Focuses on composite ML-KEM for use in X.509 certificates and CMS.
  • draft-ietf-lamps-pq-composite-sigs-02: Addresses composite ML-DSA for use in Internet PKI.

CycloneDX SBOMs: Version 1.6 of CycloneDX SBOMs fully supports CBOMs as of April 2024, providing a standardized framework for documenting cryptographic assets.

The transition to quantum-safe digital certificates requires careful planning and execution. Enterprises must assess their specific needs and risk profiles, stay informed about evolving standards and best practices, and actively engage with vendors and industry partners to ensure a successful and secure migration to a quantum-resistant infrastructure.
