The Quantum Threat to Crypto Assets

The potential for quantum computers to break current cryptographic systems poses a significant threat to the crypto-assets industry, including central bank digital currencies (CBDCs). Here's a breakdown of the impact:

Quantum Computers Could Undermine Security

  • Breaking Digital Signatures:

    • Most cryptocurrencies, including Bitcoin, use digital signatures to verify transactions and prevent double-spending. [1, 2]
    • These signatures rely on algorithms like ECDSA, which are vulnerable to attacks by quantum computers using Shor's algorithm. [3, 4]
    • If an attacker with a quantum computer could break these signatures, they could potentially steal crypto assets or manipulate transactions on the blockchain. [5]

  • Weakening Hashing Algorithms:

    • Quantum computers could also weaken the hashing algorithms used to secure blockchains and crypto wallets. [3]
    • While not as immediately threatened as digital signatures, the weakening of hashing algorithms could still impact the security of these systems. [4]

  • Harvesting Encrypted Data:

    • Threat actors could use a "harvest now, decrypt later" strategy, collecting encrypted data today and decrypting it later using a powerful quantum computer. [6-9]
    • This strategy poses a significant risk to the long-term security of crypto assets, as it means that data encrypted today could be compromised in the future. [6, 7, 9]

Impact on CBDCs

  • CBDCs, like other crypto assets, are vulnerable to the same quantum threats. [10, 11]
  • Given that CBDCs are likely to be issued and managed by central banks, the quantum threat could have serious implications for financial stability and national security. [12]
  • A successful quantum attack on a CBDC could undermine trust in the currency and disrupt the financial system. [13]

Mitigating the Quantum Threat

  • Transitioning to Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC):

    • PQC uses algorithms that are resistant to both classical and quantum computers. [14, 17]
    • Organizations involved with crypto assets, including central banks developing CBDCs, need to begin planning and implementing a transition to PQC. [14-24]
    • This transition will likely be complex and time-consuming, requiring careful planning and execution. [14, 15, 20, 25, 26]

  • Hybrid Approaches:

    • Combining PQC with existing cryptographic algorithms can provide enhanced security during the transition. [27]
    • This approach can help to mitigate the risk of unforeseen vulnerabilities in PQC algorithms while allowing organizations to gradually migrate to fully quantum-resistant systems. [27, 28]

  • Quantum-Resistant Blockchain Architectures:

    • Research is exploring the development of blockchain designs that are inherently resistant to quantum attacks. [24, 29]
    • This includes exploring concepts like "quantum blockchains," which leverage quantum phenomena for enhanced security. [29]

Urgency of Action

While the exact timeline for the development of cryptographically relevant quantum computers is uncertain, experts predict that they could be available within the next decade. [1, 5, 30-36]

Given the potential for data harvesting attacks, the quantum threat is not just a future problem but a present concern. [7, 9, 20]

The crypto asset industry, including those developing CBDCs, needs to act now to prepare for the quantum threat and protect the integrity and security of their systems. [7, 9, 11, 13, 14, 20, 22, 23]

The quantum threat is a serious challenge for the crypto asset industry. By taking proactive steps to mitigate the threat, the industry can ensure its long-term security and stability.
